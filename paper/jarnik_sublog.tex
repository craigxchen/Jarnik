\title{A Sublogarithmic Bound for Lattice Points\\ on Critical Circular Arcs}
\author{Craig Chen}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
Let
\[
  \E_N=\{(x,y)\in\Z^2:x^2+y^2=N\}.
\]
When \(N=R^2\), this is the set of lattice points on the circle of radius
\(R\).  We prove that for every fixed \(C>0\) and every \(D>50\), all
sufficiently large radii \(R\) with \(R^2\in\N\), and every arc of length at
most \(C\sqrt R\) on \(x^2+y^2=R^2\), the number of lattice points on the arc
is at most
\[
  D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
The proof is arithmetic rather than purely convex-geometric. It combines a
Ramana-type determinant identity over \(\Z[i]\), congruence collisions at
rational primes missing from the norm, a Chebyshev interval lower bound, and
split/inert descent in the Gaussian integers.  The Lean development proves a
parametrized chord-distance theorem from which the usual arc statement follows.
\end{abstract}

\noindent\textbf{Keywords.}
lattice points on circles; short arcs; Gaussian integers; determinant method;
restriction estimates; Lean 4.

\section{Introduction}

The distribution of lattice points on circles is an old problem with a
distinctive feature: globally the number of representations
\[
  x^2+y^2=N
\]
can fluctuate arithmetically, but locally on a short arc one expects much more
rigidity.  The guiding question is the following.  If \(R^2=N\), how many
points of \(\E_N\) can lie on an arc of length comparable to \(\sqrt R\)?

For a beginning number theorist, the tension is useful to keep in mind.  The
set \(\E_N\) is not a random set of points on a circle; it is controlled by the
factorization of \(N\) in the Gaussian integers.  At the same time, once we zoom
into a very short arc, the problem looks geometric: many nearby lattice points
would force many small chord distances.  The proof below exploits exactly this
meeting point between local geometry and arithmetic factorization.

The scale \(\sqrt R\) is critical for the circle.  It is much shorter than the
radius, so the arc is locally flat at first sight, but it is long enough that
the usual convexity bounds are far from sharp.  It is also the scale which
appears in questions about concentration of Fourier modes of toral
eigenfunctions: the frequency set of an eigenfunction with eigenvalue
\(4\pi^2N\) is precisely \(\E_N\), and clusters of frequencies in short arcs
influence restriction and oscillatory-kernel estimates.

It is conjectured that arcs of length comparable to \(R^{1/2}\) contain only
boundedly many lattice points, uniformly in the radius \(R\),
\cite{CillerueloGranville2007,CillerueloGranville2009}.  In this paper we
prove the weaker sublogarithmic estimate
\[
  O_C\!\left(\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}\right).
\]
This improves the logarithmic bound obtained by choosing the number of points
in the Cilleruelo--Cordoba fixed-\(k\) estimates as a function of \(R\).

This question was the starting point of an earlier expository project of the
author on lattice points on circles \cite{Chen2021}, which surveyed
Jarn\'ik's theorem, the elementary triangle-area argument bounding the number
of points on an arc of length \(R^{1/3}\), and the Cilleruelo--Cordoba
determinant method underlying the present paper.  What follows formalizes and
substantially strengthens that survey's estimates at the critical scale
\(\sqrt R\), in a proof checked end to end by a proof assistant rather than
by hand.

\subsection{Notation}

We write \(f\ll g\), or \(f=O(g)\), to mean that there is an absolute
constant \(c\) with \(|f|\le c\,g\) for all values of the relevant
parameters; a subscript, as in \(f\ll_C g\) or \(f=O_C(g)\), means the
implied constant is allowed to depend on \(C\) but on nothing else.  All
implied constants below are effective -- nothing is proved by a
non-constructive compactness argument -- though we have made no attempt to
optimize them.  We identify a point \((x,y)\in\Z^2\) with the Gaussian
integer \(z=x+iy\in\GI\), write \(\conj z\) for complex conjugation, and
write \(\Norm(z)=z\conj z=x^2+y^2\) for the norm, which is completely
multiplicative on \(\GI\).

The proof should be thought of as a determinant method with extra arithmetic
divisibility.  A Vandermonde-type determinant over \(\Z[i]\) cannot vanish when
we feed it distinct points on the circle, but if many consecutive points lie in
a short parameter interval then the same determinant is forced to be small.
This gives the first obstruction to clustering.  The logarithmic saving comes
from strengthening the determinant with congruences modulo rational primes.
Primes \(p\nmid N\) give collision divisibility, while primes dividing \(N\)
allow descent to a smaller Gaussian-integer circle.  Organizing these two
alternatives over a geometric stack of prime intervals produces the
\(\log\log R\) gain.

\subsection{Main result}

\begin{theorem}[Geometric arc form]\label{thm:geometric}
For every \(C>0\) and \(D>50\) there exists \(R_0(C,D)\) such that the
following holds.  Let \(R\ge R_0(C,D)\), let \(N\in\N\) satisfy \(R^2=N\), and
let \(I\) be an arc of the circle \(x^2+y^2=N\) of Euclidean length at most
\(C\sqrt R\).  Then
\[
  \#(I\cap\Z^2)
  \le
  D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{remark}
The threshold \(D>50\) in Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric} is an explicit,
formally verified constant, not a claim of sharpness; we discuss exactly
where it comes from in Section~\ref{subsec:cc-log}, and where it might be
improved in Remark~\ref{rem:constant-not-sharp}.  What matters for the
argument is only that some absolute \(D\) works, uniformly in \(C\).
\end{remark}

The theorem formalized in Lean is slightly more general and avoids a
topological formalization of arcs.  It works with ordered real parameters whose
differences dominate chord lengths.

\begin{theorem}[Formalized asymptotic parametrized theorem]\label{thm:param}
Let \(C>0\) and \(D>50\).  There exists \(R_0(C,D)\) such that for every
\(R\ge R_0(C,D)\), the following holds.  Let
\(M,N\in\N\), \(a,L\in\R\), and let \(z:\N\to\GI\), \(t:\N\to\R\).  Assume
\[
  R^2=N,\qquad 0<M,\qquad 0<N,\qquad L\le C\sqrt R,
\]
\[
  z_i\conj{z_i}=N\quad\text{for all }i<M,
\]
that \(z_i\ne z_j\) for distinct \(i,j<M\), that \(t_i\le t_j\) whenever
\(i\le j<M\), that
\[
  |z_i-z_j|^2\le (t_j-t_i)^2\qquad (i,j<M),
\]
and that
\[
  a\le t_i\le a+L\qquad (i<M).
\]
Then
\[
  M\le D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{theorem}

In Lean this is
\begin{center}
\texttt{GaussianChain.AsymptoticParameters.}\\
\texttt{eventually\_jarnik\_arc\_sublog\_}\\
\texttt{of\_constant\_gt\_fifty}.
\end{center}
The hypotheses named \texttt{OnCircleUpTo M N z} and
\texttt{InjectiveUpTo M z} in Lean describe the \(M\) points being counted at a
fixed radius.  The theorem itself is asymptotic in \(R\): after the threshold
\(R_0(C,D)\), it applies uniformly to every such \(M\)-point list at that
radius.  This formulation avoids the false requirement that an infinite
sequence of distinct points lie on one fixed circle.
The implication Theorem~\ref{thm:param}\(\Rightarrow\)Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric}
is immediate by taking \(t_i\) to be arclength along the arc: chord distance is
bounded by arclength distance.

\subsection{Proof idea}

We now give a more explicit roadmap.  The reader should view \(s\) as the
window size parameter; eventually \(s\) will be chosen on the order of
\(\log R/\log\log R\).

First, a determinant identity over \(\GI\) gives a subcritical obstruction.  In
any block of \(2s+1\) distinct points on \(x^2+y^2=N\), a Ramana-type
determinant factors into a power of the common norm and a Vandermonde product.
Since the points are distinct, the determinant is nonzero, and hence its norm is
at least \(1\).  If every squared chord length in the block is at most \(B\),
this gives
\[
  N^{s^2}\le B^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
Thus, if \(B^{s(2s+1)}<N^{s^2}\), every consecutive \(2s+1\)-point block must
have a large parameter span.  Sliding windows convert that local obstruction
into a cardinality bound.

Second, the determinant can be strengthened using primes \(p\nmid N\).  Modulo
such a prime, the equation \(x^2+y^2=N\) has only \(O(p)\) possible residue
classes.  A block of \(2s+1\) points therefore has many pairs which collide
modulo \(p\).  Each collision contributes a factor of \(p\) to the determinant.
If many primes in a controlled interval are missing from \(N\), the accumulated
prime-power divisibility makes the determinant too divisible to be compatible
with all chords being small.  This is the missing-prime branch.

Third, if the missing-prime branch fails, then many primes in the same interval
must divide \(N\).  In particular, one finds a prime divisor \(p\) of \(N\) in a
controlled range.  The arithmetic of \(\Z[i]\) then gives descent.  If
\(p\equiv 3\pmod 4\), the rational prime is inert and divides every point on the
circle when it divides the norm.  If \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\), the prime splits as
\(p=\pi\overline\pi\), and at least half of the points are divisible by one of
\(\pi,\overline\pi\).  Dividing by the relevant factor maps the chosen points to
a smaller circle and shrinks chord lengths.  The determinant obstruction can
then be applied on the descended circle.

The final proof balances these two alternatives over a geometric stack of prime
intervals.  Either sufficiently many primes are missing, giving extra
determinant divisibility, or a useful prime divisor of \(N\) appears, giving
descent.  Choosing the window parameter \(s\) at scale
\(\log R/\log\log R\) makes the two mechanisms meet, and this is where the
sublogarithmic bound comes from.

\section{Related Work and the Logarithmic Baseline}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Classical convex-curve bounds}

Jarnik's theorem on lattice points on convex curves gives the correct order of
magnitude \(O(L^{2/3})\) for lattice points on a strictly convex arc of length
\(L\) \cite{Jarnik1926}.  Applied directly to a circular arc of length
\(L=C\sqrt R\), this gives only \(O_C(R^{1/3})\).  Bombieri and Pila's
determinant method gives far-reaching bounds for integral points on arcs and
ovals \cite{BombieriPila1989}; our proof is also determinant-driven, but it
uses the special arithmetic of a fixed circle and the Gaussian integers.

\subsection{Cilleruelo--Cordoba and Cilleruelo--Granville}

For circles centered at the origin, much sharper local results are known.
Cilleruelo and Cordoba proved fixed-\(k\) bounds below the square-root scale
\cite{CillerueloCordoba1992}.  A convenient form is:
for every \(k\ge 1\), every arc of length
\[
  \sqrt2\,R^{1/2-\frac{1}{4\floor{k/2}+2}}
\]
contains at most \(k\) lattice points.  Cilleruelo and Granville later studied
the fine structure of close lattice points on circles and gave families of four
points at short scales \cite{CillerueloGranville2009}; related conjectures on
boundedness of short arcs appear in \cite{CillerueloGranville2007}.

\subsection{Deriving the implied logarithmic bound}
\label{subsec:cc-log}

The Cilleruelo--Cordoba estimates imply a logarithmic bound at the critical
\(\sqrt R\)-scale.  We include the derivation because it is the clean baseline
improved by Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric}.

Assume \(R>e^2\), and set
\[
  k_R=\ceil{\frac{\log R}{2}}.
\]
Then
\[
  4\floor{k_R/2}+2\ge \log R.
\]
Indeed, if \(k_R=2q\), then \(2q\ge \frac12\log R\), so
\(4q+2\ge \log R+2\).  If \(k_R=2q+1\), then
\(2q+1\ge \frac12\log R\), so \(4q+2\ge \log R\).  Therefore
\[
  \frac{1}{4\floor{k_R/2}+2}\le \frac1{\log R}.
\]
The length allowed by the Cilleruelo--Cordoba theorem is at least
\[
  \sqrt2\,R^{1/2-\frac{1}{4\floor{k_R/2}+2}}
  \ge
  \sqrt2\,R^{1/2-\frac1{\log R}}
  =
  \frac{\sqrt2}{e}\sqrt R.
\]
Thus arcs of length at most \((\sqrt2/e)\sqrt R\) contain at most
\[
  k_R\le \frac12\log R+1
\]
lattice points.

For a general fixed \(C>0\), divide an arc of length at most \(C\sqrt R\) into
\[
  J_C=\ceil{\frac{Ce}{\sqrt2}}
\]
subarcs, each of length at most \((\sqrt2/e)\sqrt R\).  Summing the preceding
bound over the subarcs gives
\[
  \#(I\cap\Z^2)
  \le
  J_C\left(\frac12\log R+1\right)
  =
  O_C(\log R).
\]
Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric} replaces this \(O_C(\log R)\) baseline by
\[
  O_C\!\left(\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}\right).
\]

\subsection{Restriction estimates}

The same local-clustering question enters restriction estimates for toral
eigenfunctions.  If
\[
  \varphi(x)=\sum_{\mu\in\E_N}a_\mu e^{2\pi i\langle \mu,x\rangle}
\]
is an eigenfunction on \(\T^2\), then restriction of \(\varphi\) to a curved
hypersurface is controlled by oscillatory sums over pairs of frequencies.
Near-parallel frequencies, which are precisely frequencies lying in short arcs
of \(\E_N\), form the difficult local contribution.  Bourgain and Rudnick
proved uniform restriction bounds for curves with nonzero curvature
\cite{BourgainRudnick2009}.  The present theorem gives a formalized
sublogarithmic local multiplicity estimate at the critical scale, and therefore
supplies a quantitative input for any restriction argument whose loss is
controlled by such arc-clustering numbers.

\section{Main Results Used in the Proof}

This section states the principal ingredients.  Full proofs are given in the
appendix.

\subsection{The Ramana determinant}

Let \(s\ge 1\), and let \(z_0,\dots,z_{2s}\in\GI\).  Define the
\((2s+1)\times(2s+1)\) matrix
\[
  \mathcal R_s(z)_{r,j} =
  \begin{cases}
    \conj{z_j}^{\,s-r}, & 0\le r<s,\\
    1, & r=s,\\
    z_j^{\,r-s}, & s<r\le 2s.
  \end{cases}
\]
Let
\[
  J_s(z_0,\ldots,z_{2s})=\det \mathcal R_s(z).
\]

\begin{proposition}[Ramana determinant identity]\label{prop:ramana}
If \(z_j\conj z_j=N\) for every \(j\), then
\[
  \left(\prod_{j=0}^{2s}z_j^s\right)J_s(z_0,\ldots,z_{2s})
  =
  N^{s(s+1)/2}\prod_{0\le i<j\le 2s}(z_j-z_i).
\]
If the \(z_j\) are distinct, then \(J_s\ne 0\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Subcritical block obstruction]\label{cor:subcritical}
Let \(z_0,\dots,z_{2s}\in\GI\) be distinct and satisfy \(z_j\conj z_j=N>0\).
If \(\Norm(z_i-z_j)\le B\) for every \(i<j\), then
\[
  N^{s^2}\le B^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
Consequently, if \(B^{s(2s+1)}<N^{s^2}\), some pair in the block has squared
distance \(>B\).
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Sliding windows}

\begin{lemma}[Window-to-cardinality bound]\label{lem:window}
Let
\[
  t_0\le t_1\le\cdots\le t_{M-1}
\]
lie in an interval of length \(L\).  Suppose \(A>0\), \(s\ge 1\), and every
consecutive window of \(2s+1\) indices has span greater than \(A\):
\[
  t_{j+2s}-t_j>A\qquad (0\le j<M-2s).
\]
Then
\[
  M\le 2s+\frac{2sL}{A}.
\]
\end{lemma}

Together with Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical}, this proves a bound whenever the
determinant inequality is already subcritical at the original scale.

\subsection{Missing primes}

For \(m<U\), define the weighted missing-prime mass
\[
  \M_N(m,U)
  =
  \sum_{\substack{m<p\le U\\p\text{ prime}\\p\nmid N}}\frac{\log p}{p}.
\]
The Lean proof uses a finite weighted-log condition:
\[
  s(2s+1)\log \floor{A^2}
  <
  s^2\M_N(m,U)-2\sum_{\substack{m<p\le U\\p\nmid N}}\log p
  +s^2\log N.
  \tag{MP}\label{eq:missing-prime-condition}
\]
The exact Lean statement uses natural-number floors and coercions; this display
is its mathematical content.

\begin{proposition}[Missing-prime branch]\label{prop:missing}
Assume \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition}.  Then the parametrized family
satisfies
\[
  M\le 2s+\frac{2sL}{A}.
\]
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Prime extraction and descent}

Let \(m_0\ge 2\), \(k>0\), and set
\[
  U=m_0\,8^k.
\]
The prime-stack condition is:
\[
  \M_N(m_0,U)<k\,\frac{\log 2}{2}.
  \tag{FP}\label{eq:few-primes}
\]
Using Chebyshev's lower bound on prime mass in the intervals
\[
  m_0 8^i < p \le m_0 8^{i+1},
\]
condition \eqref{eq:few-primes} forces at least one prime divisor
\[
  m_0<p\le U,\qquad p\mid N.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Descent branch]\label{prop:descent}
Let \(p\mid N\) be a prime with \(m_0<p\le U\).
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item If \(p\equiv 3\pmod 4\), every \(z\in\E_N\) is divisible by \(p\) in
\(\GI\), and division by \(p\) maps the family into \(\E_{N/p^2}\).  Chord
lengths are divided by \(p\).
\item If \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\), write \(p=\pi\conj\pi\) in \(\GI\).  At least
half the points are divisible by one of \(\pi,\conj\pi\).  Dividing that
subfamily by the corresponding Gaussian prime maps it into \(\E_{N/p}\).  Chord
lengths are divided by \(\sqrt p\).
\end{enumerate}
If the corresponding descended subcritical inequalities hold, the original
family satisfies a cardinality bound of size \(O(s)\).
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Finite two-scale theorem}

The formal finite theorem packages the preceding alternatives with split and
inert scales.  In the split branch we deliberately use a constant-enlarged
subcritical scale:
\[
  A_{\mathrm{split}}(p)=48\,\frac{C\sqrt R}{\sqrt p},
  \qquad
  A_{\mathrm{inert}}(p)=\frac{C\sqrt R}{p}.
\]
\begin{remark}\label{rem:constant-not-sharp}
The factor \(48\) is not intrinsic.  It is a fixed convenience constant: it
shrinks the split sliding-window length term by \(1/48\), while the endpoint
subcritical inequality only has to absorb the fixed extra term \(2\log48\).
Together with the base-\(8\) prime stack and the specific rational
coefficients chosen in the calibration of Section~\ref{subsec:asymp} below,
it is exactly this kind of arbitrary-but-convenient choice that produces the
constant \(D>50\) in Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric} rather than something
sharper; we have prioritized a clean, mechanically checked proof over
optimizing constants, and we would be surprised if \(50\) were close to
best possible.
\end{remark}

\begin{theorem}[Finite two-scale theorem]\label{thm:finite}
Let \(R^2=N\), let \(C>0\), and let the parametrized family satisfy the
hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:param}.  Suppose \(s,m_0,k,B\) satisfy:
\[
  s\le B\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R},
\]
\[
  4m_0 8^k\le s,
\]
the Chebyshev error bounds on the stack intervals, the missing-prime threshold
inequality, the split endpoint subcritical inequality with \(48C\) in place of
\(C\), and the inert endpoint subcritical inequality with \(C\).  Then
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}B\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{theorem}

This is the theorem named
\[
\texttt{card\_le\_49\_div\_12\_mul\_log\_div\_loglog\_}\\
\texttt{of\_radius\_sq\_twoScale\_endpoint\_bounds}
\]
in \texttt{GaussianChain/MainSublogBound.lean}.

\subsection{Asymptotic parameters}\label{subsec:asymp}

The final choice is
\[
  W=\log R,\qquad \Lambda=\log W,
\]
\[
  s=\ceil{\frac{97}{8}\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}},\qquad
  m_0=\floor{\sqrt W},\qquad
  k=\floor{\frac{\Lambda}{(2001/1000)\log 8}}.
\]
For every \(B>97/8\), the ceiling loss is eventually absorbed:
\[
  s\le B\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}.
\]
Taking \(B=600/49\) in Theorem~\ref{thm:finite} gives the concrete corollary
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}\cdot \frac{600}{49}\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}
  =
  50\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
Taking \(B=12D/49\) gives the stated theorem for every \(D>50\).

The constants are chosen to make the threshold inequality just transparent.
Let
\[
  \alpha=\frac{2001}{1000},\qquad
  \beta=\frac{201}{100}.
\]
The upper estimate for the stack gives
\[
  8^k\le W^{1/\alpha}=W^{1000/2001},
\]
and hence
\[
  U=m_0 8^k\le W^{1/2}W^{1000/2001}=W^{4001/4002}.
\]
Since \(\Lambda=o(W^{1/4002})\), this implies \(4U\le s\) for large \(R\).

For the lower estimate on \(k\), the floor loss is handled with a second
constant \(\beta>\alpha\).  If
\[
  x=\frac{\Lambda}{\alpha\log8},
\]
then \(k=\floor{x}\), and for large \(x\),
\[
  \floor{x}\ge x-1\ge \frac{\alpha}{\beta}x
  =
  \frac{\Lambda}{\beta\log8}.
\]
Therefore
\[
  k\,\frac{\log2}{2}
  \ge
  \frac{\Lambda}{\beta\log8}\,\frac{\log2}{2}
  =
  \frac{1}{6\beta}\Lambda
  =
  \frac{50}{603}\Lambda.
\]
This is where the earlier \(12\alpha\) denominator came from: using only
\(\floor{x}\ge x/2\) is the special case \(\beta=2\alpha\), which gives the
weaker gain \(\Lambda/(12\alpha)\).  The sharper eventual floor estimate
recovers nearly the full stack height and changes the balance to
\(\Lambda/(6\beta)\).

The main error from replacing \(s\) by its lower bound is
\[
  sW\le \frac{8}{97}s^2\Lambda.
\]
The remaining endpoint and fixed-\(C\) terms are made negligible:
\[
  2(U+1)\log U\le \frac{1}{8192}s^2\Lambda,
  \qquad
  (s(2s+1))(2\log C)\le \frac{3}{524288}s^2\Lambda.
\]
For the enlarged split scale, the same endpoint estimate is applied with
\(48C\) in place of \(C\).  This only changes the fixed constant term from
\(2\log C\) to \(2\log(48C)\), which is still absorbed by \(\Lambda\) for
fixed \(C\).
The exact numerical comparison used in Lean is
\[
  \frac{8}{97}+\frac1{8192}+\frac{3}{524288}
  <
  \frac{50}{603}.
\]
Thus the stack gain absorbs the threshold errors.

\section{Conclusion and Restriction Consequences}

The theorem gives a formalized improvement from the Cilleruelo--Cordoba
logarithmic baseline
\[
  O_C(\log R)
\]
to
\[
  O_C\!\left(\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}\right)
\]
for arcs of length \(C\sqrt R\).  It does not settle the stronger endpoint
boundedness conjecture.  The point is different: the argument gives a verified
sublogarithmic local-multiplicity estimate at the exact scale which is relevant
to arithmetic concentration phenomena.

For restriction problems on \(\T^2\), the frequency set of an eigenfunction is
\(\E_N\).  Restriction to a curved hypersurface leads to kernels of the form
\[
  K(s,t)=\sum_{\mu,\nu\in\E_N}
  a_\mu\overline{a_\nu}\,
  e^{2\pi i\langle \mu-\nu,\gamma(s)-\gamma(t)\rangle}.
\]
Pairs \((\mu,\nu)\) with nearly parallel directions are the part least affected
by curvature and stationary phase.  The number of such frequencies is controlled
by short-arc multiplicity.  Therefore any restriction argument whose local loss
is bounded by the maximal number of lattice points in arcs of length
\(C\sqrt R\) may replace a logarithmic loss by
\[
  \frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
In this sense Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric} supplies a formal arithmetic input for
restriction estimates: it quantifies, in a machine-checked way, that critical
frequency clusters are sublogarithmic.  It should not be read as a standalone
new restriction theorem; translating the local cluster estimate into a sharp
operator norm requires the analytic restriction framework in which the cluster
number appears.

\begin{remark}
This restriction connection is not an afterthought: it is the reason the
problem was interesting to the author in the first place.  In
\cite{Chen2021}, the same set \(\E_N\) of lattice points on a circle appears
as the frequency support of a toral eigenfunction, and counting points on
short arcs is exactly what controls how badly that eigenfunction's energy can
concentrate along a one-dimensional curve -- the question underlying local
well-posedness for the nonlinear Schr\"odinger equation on the torus.  The
present paper answers, with a machine-checked proof, a sharper version of the
counting question that survey could only state.
\end{remark}

\section{Proofs of the Geometric and Related-Work Statements}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric} from
Theorem~\ref{thm:param}]
Let \(R^2=N\).  Let \(I\) be an arc of length at most \(C\sqrt R\), and
enumerate the lattice points on \(I\) in increasing arclength order:
\[
  z_0,\dots,z_{M-1}.
\]
Then \(z_i\conj{z_i}=N\) for every \(i<M\), and the enumerated points are
pairwise distinct.
Let \(t_i\) be the arclength coordinate of \(z_i\) measured from the initial
endpoint of the arc.  Then \(0\le t_i\le L\le C\sqrt R\), and
\[
  t_i\le t_j\qquad (i\le j).
\]
Since Euclidean chord length is at most arclength along a circle,
\[
  |z_i-z_j|\le |t_j-t_i|.
\]
Squaring gives the chord hypothesis in Theorem~\ref{thm:param}.  The points are
distinct by enumeration, so the finite distinctness hypothesis holds.  Applying
Theorem~\ref{thm:param} gives the claimed bound.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of the Cilleruelo--Cordoba logarithmic consequence]
This is the derivation in Section~\ref{subsec:cc-log}.  Choose
\[
  k_R=\ceil{\frac{\log R}{2}}.
\]
Then \(4\floor{k_R/2}+2\ge\log R\), hence
\[
  R^{1/2-\frac{1}{4\floor{k_R/2}+2}}
  \ge R^{1/2-\frac{1}{\log R}}
  =e^{-1}\sqrt R.
\]
Therefore the \(k_R\)-point Cilleruelo--Cordoba theorem applies to every arc of
length at most \((\sqrt2/e)\sqrt R\), giving at most \(k_R\le \frac12\log R+1\)
points.  A general \(C\sqrt R\)-arc is covered by
\[
  \ceil{Ce/\sqrt2}
\]
such arcs, so the total number of points is
\[
  O_C(\log R).
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Determinant and Subcritical Bounds}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:ramana}]
The \(j\)-th column of \(\mathcal R_s\) is
\[
  (\conj z_j^s,\conj z_j^{s-1},\ldots,\conj z_j,1,z_j,\ldots,z_j^s)^T.
\]
Multiply this column by \(z_j^s\).  Because \(z_j\conj z_j=N\), the entries
become
\[
  (N^s,N^{s-1}z_j,\ldots,Nz_j^{s-1},z_j^s,z_j^{s+1},\ldots,z_j^{2s})^T.
\]
Factor \(N^{s-r}\) from the row corresponding to \(\conj z^{s-r}\), for
\(r=0,\dots,s-1\).  The total factored power is
\[
  N^{1+2+\cdots+s}=N^{s(s+1)/2}.
\]
The remaining matrix is the ordinary Vandermonde matrix with columns
\[
  (1,z_j,z_j^2,\ldots,z_j^{2s})^T.
\]
Its determinant is
\[
  \prod_{0\le i<j\le 2s}(z_j-z_i).
\]
This proves the identity.  If the \(z_j\) are distinct, the Vandermonde product
is nonzero in the domain \(\GI\), and the product \(\prod_j z_j^s\) is nonzero
because \(N>0\).  Hence \(J_s\ne0\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical}]
Take norms in Proposition~\ref{prop:ramana}.  Since \(J_s\) is a nonzero
Gaussian integer,
\[
  \Norm(J_s)\ge 1.
\]
The norm of \(\prod_j z_j^s\) is
\[
  \prod_j \Norm(z_j)^s=N^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
The norm of the right side is
\[
  \Norm\!\left(N^{s(s+1)/2}\right)
  \prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i)
  =
  N^{s(s+1)}\prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i),
\]
because the rational integer \(N^{s(s+1)/2}\) has Gaussian norm
\(N^{s(s+1)}\).  Thus
\[
  N^{s(2s+1)}
  \le
  N^{s(s+1)}\prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i),
\]
and hence
\[
  N^{s^2}\le \prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i).
\]
There are
\[
  \binom{2s+1}{2}=s(2s+1)
\]
pairs.  If every norm is at most \(B\), then
\[
  N^{s^2}\le B^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
The final assertion is the contrapositive.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:window}]
Let \(K=2s\).  The hypothesis gives
\[
  t_{j+K}-t_j>A\qquad (0\le j<M-K).
\]
Sum these inequalities over \(j=0,\dots,M-K-1\):
\[
  (M-K)A
  <
  \sum_{j=0}^{M-K-1}(t_{j+K}-t_j).
\]
Each \(t_i\) occurs with coefficient at most \(K\) in the telescoping
difference between the final and initial portions of the sequence, and all
\(t_i\) lie in an interval of length \(L\).  Hence
\[
  \sum_{j=0}^{M-K-1}(t_{j+K}-t_j)\le KL.
\]
Therefore
\[
  M-K < \frac{KL}{A},
\]
and so
\[
  M\le K+\frac{KL}{A}
  =
  2s+\frac{2sL}{A}.
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Missing-Prime and Descent Mechanisms}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:missing}]
For a prime \(p\nmid N\), the congruence
\[
  x^2+y^2\equiv N\pmod p
\]
has at most \(2p\) solutions as \(x\) varies: for each \(x\), the value of
\(y^2\) is determined, and a quadratic congruence has at most two roots.
Therefore a block of points on the circle has many pairs congruent modulo
\(p\).  Whenever \(z_i\equiv z_j\pmod p\), the Gaussian integer
\(z_j-z_i\) is divisible by \(p\).  Proposition~\ref{prop:ramana} expresses
\(J_s\) in terms of the product of all \(z_j-z_i\), so these collisions force a
power of \(p\) to divide \(J_s\).

Summing the \(p\)-adic contributions over \(m<p\le U\), \(p\nmid N\), gives a
lower bound for the norm of the determinant in terms of
\(\M_N(m,U)\).  The term
\[
  2\sum_{\substack{m<p\le U\\p\nmid N}}\log p
\]
is the finite error coming from the collision count and from replacing exact
valuations by weighted lower bounds.  If \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition}
holds, the determinant cannot remain small when all chord lengths in a window
are \(\le A\).  Thus every \(2s+1\)-point window has span \(>A\), and
Lemma~\ref{lem:window} gives
\[
  M\le 2s+\frac{2sL}{A}.
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:descent}]
If \(p\equiv3\pmod4\), then \(-1\) is not a square modulo \(p\).  From
\[
  x^2+y^2\equiv0\pmod p
\]
we obtain \(x\equiv y\equiv0\pmod p\).  Hence \(p\mid z\) in \(\GI\) for every
point \(z\), and \(z/p\in\GI\) satisfies
\[
  \Norm(z/p)=N/p^2.
\]
Moreover
\[
  |z_i/p-z_j/p|=|z_i-z_j|/p.
\]
Thus the parameter length in the descended problem is divided by \(p\).

If \(p\equiv1\pmod4\), then \(p=\pi\conj\pi\) in \(\GI\).  Since
\[
  p\mid z\conj z,
\]
at least one of \(\pi,\conj\pi\) divides \(z\).  Partition the original family
according to which Gaussian prime divides \(z\); one class has at least half the
points.  Divide that class by the corresponding Gaussian prime.  The norm drops
from \(N\) to \(N/p\), and chord lengths are divided by
\[
  |\pi|=\sqrt p.
\]

In either case, once the descended determinant inequality is subcritical,
Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical} and Lemma~\ref{lem:window} apply on the
descended circle.  In the split case the passage to the larger of two classes
costs a genuine factor of \(2\).  The optimized finite theorem keeps this
factor but reduces the split sliding-window length term by using a constant
enlargement of the descended subcritical scale.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Finite Two-Scale Theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:finite}]
Let
\[
  U=m_0\,8^k.
\]
There are two cases.

If the missing-prime mass on \((m_0,U]\) is at least
\[
  k\,\frac{\log2}{2},
\]
then the threshold hypothesis implies the weighted missing-prime condition
\eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition}.  Proposition~\ref{prop:missing} gives
\[
  M\le 2s+\frac{2sL}{C\sqrt R}.
\]
Since \(L\le C\sqrt R\), this is at most \(4s\), and hence at most
\[
  \frac{49}{12}B\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}
\]
under the hypothesis \(s\le B\log R/\log\log R\).

If the missing-prime mass is smaller than \(k\log2/2\), the Chebyshev interval
lower bound over the geometric stack implies that some prime \(p\mid N\) lies
in \(m_0<p\le U\).  Apply Proposition~\ref{prop:descent}.  The finite theorem
assumes the split endpoint inequality
\[
  s(2s+1)\log\left(\frac{(48C)^2R}{m_0}\right)
  <
  s^2(2\log R-\log m_0),
\]
and the inert endpoint inequality
\[
  s(2s+1)\log\left(\frac{C^2R}{m_0^2}\right)
  <
  s^2(2\log R-2\log m_0).
\]
Since \(p>m_0\), these endpoint inequalities imply the corresponding descended
subcritical inequalities for every possible \(p\) in the stack.  The descended
window bound gives at most \(4s\) points in the inert case.  In the split case
we use the scale
\[
  48\,\frac{C\sqrt R}{\sqrt p}.
\]
The descended length is at most \(C\sqrt R/\sqrt p\), so the sliding-window
length term is only \(1/48\) of the base term:
\[
  \frac{2s(C\sqrt R/\sqrt p)}{48C\sqrt R/\sqrt p}
  =
  \frac{2s}{48}.
\]
The descended half-subfamily therefore has size at most
\[
  2s+\frac{2s}{48}=\frac{49}{24}s.
\]
Multiplying by \(2\) for the split divisibility class gives
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}s.
\]
Thus in all cases
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}s\le \frac{49}{12}B\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Asymptotic Parameter Estimates}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:param}]
Let
\[
  W=\log R,\qquad \Lambda=\log W.
\]
For sufficiently large \(R\), both \(W\) and \(\Lambda\) are positive.  Choose
\[
  s=\ceil{\frac{97}{8}\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}},\qquad
  m_0=\floor{\sqrt W},\qquad
  k=\floor{\frac{\Lambda}{(2001/1000)\log8}}.
\]
Then eventually \(k>0\), \(m_0\ge2\), and
\[
  s\le 26\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}.
\]
This coarse upper bound is used only in scale estimates.  For the final
constant, the sharper fact is that \(s\le B W/\Lambda\) eventually for every
\(B>97/8\).
The prime stack endpoint satisfies
\[
  U=m_0 8^k
  \le
  W^{1/2}W^{1000/2001}
  =
  W^{4001/4002}
\]
eventually.  Since \(\Lambda=o(W^{1/4002})\),
\[
  4U\le s
\]
for all sufficiently large \(R\).

The split and inert lower-endpoint inequalities follow from
\[
  \log m_0\ge \frac{\Lambda}{4}
\]
eventually, together with
\[
  \frac{97}{8}W\le s\Lambda.
\]
The terms involving \(\log C\), and in the split branch the term
\(\log(48C)\), are fixed once \(C\) is fixed, so they are absorbed for
sufficiently large \(R\).  In the formal proof this is done with
\[
  2\log(48C)\le \frac{\Lambda}{64}
\]
leaving the numerical margin
\[
  \frac{8}{97}+\frac{3}{64}<\frac14.
\]

The missing-prime threshold is the tightest estimate.  The proof uses
\[
  2\log C\le \frac{\Lambda}{524288}
\]
eventually and the stack bound
\[
  U+1\le \frac{65}{256}s.
\]
Unlike the earlier crude estimate, we do not bound \(\log U\) by \(W=\log R\).
From \(U\le 26W\) and \(W\) large, we have \(U\le W^2\), hence
\[
  \log U\le 2\Lambda.
\]
Together with \(s\ge8320\), this gives the negligible endpoint estimate
\[
  2(U+1)\log U
  \le
  \frac1{8192}s^2\Lambda.
\]
Combining this with \((97/8)W\le s\Lambda\) yields
\[
  \left(\frac8{97}+\frac1{8192}+\frac{3}{524288}\right)s^2\Lambda
  <
  \frac{50}{603}s^2\Lambda.
\]
On the other hand,
\[
  k\,\frac{\log2}{2}\ge \frac{50}{603}\Lambda
\]
eventually.  Here we use the sharper floor estimate
\[
  \floor{\frac{\Lambda}{(2001/1000)\log8}}
  \ge
  \frac{\Lambda}{(201/100)\log8}
\]
for large \(R\).  Multiplication by \(\log2/2\), and the identity
\(\log8=3\log2\), gives \(50\Lambda/603\).  Thus the stack gain absorbs the
error term in the finite threshold inequality.

All hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:finite} are therefore satisfied.  The
estimate \(s\le (600/49)W/\Lambda\) gives the concrete bound
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}\cdot\frac{600}{49}\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}
  =
  50\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
For the sharp asymptotic constant in this fixed-parameter proof, let \(D>50\)
and put \(B=12D/49\).  Since \(B>97/8\), the ceiling loss is eventually
absorbed:
\[
  s=\ceil{(97/8)\,W/\Lambda}\le \frac{12D}{49}\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}.
\]
The finite theorem therefore gives
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}\cdot\frac{12D}{49}\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}
  =
  D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{proof}
