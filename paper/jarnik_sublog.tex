\title{A Sub-Logarithmic Bound for Lattice Points on Small Circular Arcs}
\author{Craig Chen}
\date{}

\begin{document}
\maketitle

\begin{abstract}
We prove that for a circle of radius $R$ centered at the origin, the number of lattice points on an arc of length $C\sqrt{R}$ is at most
\[
  D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}
\]
for every $C>0$ and $D>49$, once $R$ is large enough for a given $C$ and $D$.
The proof combines a Ramana-type determinant identity over \(\Z[i]\), congruence collisions at rational primes missing from the norm, a Chebyshev interval lower bound, and split/inert descent in the Gaussian integers.
The result is formalized in Lean; the formalized parameter instance certifies every $D>4753/96$.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Let $\E_N \coloneq \{ (x,y) \in \mathbb{Z}^2 \, | \, x^2 + y^2 = N \}$ be the set of lattice points on a centered circle of radius $\sqrt{N}$.
We will always assume $N=R^2$ for some $R \in \mathbb{N}$.
The problem has two faces.
Arithmetically, \(\E_N\) is controlled by the factorization of \(N\) in the Gaussian integers.
Geometrically, once we zoom into a very short arc, clustering is a statement about distances: many nearby lattice points force many small chord lengths.
We will frequently jump between these two interpretations --- identifying a lattice point \((x,y)\) with the Gaussian integer \(z=x+iy\) --- and the proof lives precisely in their interplay.

Previous results are able to prove finite bounds for arcs of length $R^{\alpha}$ when $\alpha \in [0,1/2)$ but only yield a $\log R$ bound at the critical exponent $\alpha=1/2$.
The scale \(\sqrt R\) is indeed critical for the circle.
An arc of this size is just long enough that it has nontrivial sagitta.
The usual convexity bounds are far from sharp.
And we care about results at this scale because it is also the scale which appears in questions about concentration of Fourier modes of toral eigenfunctions: the frequency set of an eigenfunction with eigenvalue \(4\pi^2N\) is precisely \(\E_N\), and clusters of frequencies in short arcs influence restriction and oscillatory-kernel estimates.

It is conjectured that arcs of length comparable to \(R^{1/2}\) also contain only finitely many lattice points, uniformly in the radius \(R\), \cite{CillerueloGranville2007,CillerueloGranville2009}.
In this paper we prove the weaker sublogarithmic estimate
\[
  O_C\!\left(\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}\right).
\]
The proof should be thought of as a determinant method with extra arithmetic divisibility.
A Vandermonde-type determinant over \(\Z[i]\) cannot vanish when we feed it distinct points on the circle, but if many consecutive points lie in a short parameter interval then the same determinant is forced to be small.
This gives the first obstruction to clustering.
The logarithmic saving comes from strengthening the determinant with congruences modulo rational primes.
Primes \(p \nmid N\) give collision divisibility, while primes dividing \(N\) allow descent to a smaller Gaussian-integer circle.
Organizing these two alternatives over a geometric stack of prime intervals produces the \(\log\log R\) gain.

\subsection{Main result}

We identify a point \((x,y)\in\Z^2\) with \(z=x+iy\in\GI\), and write \(\Norm(z)=z\conj z=x^2+y^2\).

\begin{theorem}[Geometric arc form]\label{thm:geometric}
For every \(C>0\) and \(D>49\) there exists \(R_0(C,D)\) such that the following holds.
Let \(R\ge R_0(C,D)\), let \(N\in\N\) satisfy \(R^2=N\), and let \(I\) be an arc of the circle \(x^2+y^2=N\) of length at most \(C\sqrt R\).
Then
\[
  \#(I\cap\Z^2)
  \le
  D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{theorem}

%the arithmetic input is only that the squared radius \(R^2\) is the integer
%norm \(N\).  Equivalently, one may set \(R=\sqrt N\) for an integer norm \(N\),
%in which case the length scale is \(C N^{1/4}\) and the logarithmic factor is
%\(\log\sqrt N/\log\log\sqrt N\).

The parametrized version below is slightly more general and avoids a topological formalization of arcs.
It works with ordered real parameters whose differences dominate chord lengths.

\begin{theorem}[Asymptotic parametrized theorem]\label{thm:param}
Let \(C>0\) and \(D>49\).
There exists \(R_0(C,D)\) such that for every \(R\ge R_0(C,D)\), the following holds.
Let \(M,N\in\N\), \(a,L\in\R\), and let \(z:\N\to\GI\), \(t:\N\to\R\).
Assume
\[
  R^2=N,\qquad 0<M,\qquad 0<N,\qquad L\le C\sqrt R,
\]
\[
  z_i\conj{z_i}=N\quad\text{for all }i<M,
\]
that \(z_i\ne z_j\) for distinct \(i,j<M\), that \(t_i\le t_j\) whenever \(i\le j<M\), that
\[
  |z_i-z_j|^2\le (t_j-t_i)^2\qquad (i,j<M),
\]
and that
\[
  a\le t_i\le a+L\qquad (i<M).
\]
Then
\[
  M\le D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\subsection{Proof idea}\label{subsec:proof-idea}

We now give a more explicit roadmap of the proof; precise definitions are provided in Section \ref{sec:mainresult}.
Throughout, we will try to point out the reasoning behind each choice --- most importantly the choice of the window parameter $s$, which is where the $\log/\log\log$ shape comes from.
For now, $s$ is to be thought of as a window size parameter which we will choose later; eventually \(s\) will be chosen on the order of \(\log R/\log\log R\).

First, a determinant identity over \(\GI\) gives a subcritical obstruction.
In any block of \(2s+1\) distinct points on \(x^2+y^2=N\), a Ramana-type determinant \cite{Ramana2007} factors into a power of the common norm and a Vandermonde product.
Since the points are distinct, the determinant is nonzero, and hence its norm is at least \(1\).
If every squared chord length in the block is at most \(B\), this gives
\[
  N^{s^2}\le B^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
Thus, if \(B^{s(2s+1)}<N^{s^2}\), every consecutive \(2s+1\)-point block must have a large parameter span.
Sliding windows convert that local obstruction into a cardinality bound.
These, however, are rather crude determinant bounds, and an improvement on the lower bound is key to our sublogarithmic result.

Second, we notice that the determinant carries divisibility constraints.
Take some prime \(p\nmid N\).
Modulo such a prime, the equation \(x^2+y^2=N\) has only \(O(p)\) possible residue classes.
A block of \(2s+1\) points therefore has many pairs which collide modulo \(p\).
Each collision contributes a factor of \(p\) to the determinant.
If many primes in a controlled interval are missing from \(N\), the accumulated prime-power divisibility makes the determinant too divisible to be compatible with all chords being small.
This is what we call the "missing-prime" section of the proof.

Third, if the missing-prime branch fails, then many primes in the same interval must divide \(N\).
In particular, one finds a prime divisor \(p\) of \(N\) in a controlled range.
The arithmetic of \(\Z[i]\) then gives descent.
If \(p\equiv 3\pmod 4\), the rational prime is inert and divides every point on the circle when it divides the norm.
If \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\), the prime splits as \(p=\pi\overline\pi\), and at least half of the points are divisible by one of \(\pi,\overline\pi\).
Dividing by the relevant factor maps the chosen points to a smaller circle and shrinks chord lengths.
The determinant obstruction can then be applied on the smaller circle.

The final proof balances these two alternatives over a geometric stack of prime intervals.
Either sufficiently many primes are missing, giving extra determinant divisibility, or a useful prime divisor of \(N\) appears, giving descent.
Choosing the window parameter \(s\) at scale \(\log R/\log\log R\) makes the two mechanisms meet, and this is where the sublogarithmic bound comes from.

\subsubsection*{The accounting behind \(\log R/\log\log R\)}

Where does \(\log R/\log\log R\) come from, and why can't we simply choose something finite?
It's worth making the balance quantitative before starting the proof, since the answers to both questions are visible in the bookkeeping.

Consider first the bare determinant obstruction.
Taking logarithms in the inequality \(N^{s^2}\le B^{s(2s+1)}\) with the chord bound \(B=A^2\), a \((2s+1)\)-point window of parameter span \(A\) is impossible once
\[
  s(2s+1)\log A^2<s^2\log N,
  \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad
  A<R^{\frac12-\frac{1}{4s+2}}.
\]
At the critical scale we want \(A\asymp\sqrt R\), which overshoots this threshold by the factor \(R^{1/(4s+2)}\).
Running the sliding-window bound at the largest subcritical scale instead forces
\[
  M\ \lesssim\ s+s\,C\,R^{\frac{1}{4s+2}}.
\]
For any fixed \(s\) the second term is unbounded in \(R\); it becomes \(O(1)\) only when \(s\gtrsim\log R\), and optimizing over \(s\) gives \(M=\Theta(\log R)\).
This is the same logarithmic barrier as in Section~\ref{subsec:cc-log}, now in determinant form.
In other words, in the log ledger at the scale \(A=C\sqrt R\), the arithmetic side \(s^2\log N=2s^2\log R\) falls short of the geometric side \(s(2s+1)(\log R+2\log C)\) by
\[
  s\log R+O_C(s^2),
\]
a deficit of about \((\log R)/s\) per unit of \(s^2\).
A window of fixed size carries only the fixed obstruction \(\Norm(J_s)\ge1\), so the deficit must be paid by shrinking \(A\), and this is exactly what costs us the logarithm.

The missing-prime mechanism raises the obstruction from \(\Norm(J_s)\ge1\) to \(\Norm(J_s)\ge\prod_p p^{2c_p}\), where \(c_p\) counts congruence collisions in the window.
In the ledger, each prime \(p\nmid N\) contributes about \((s^2/p)\log p\), so an interval \((m_0,m_1]\) of missing primes contributes
\[
  s^2\!\!\sum_{m_0<p\le m_1}\frac{\log p}{p}\ \approx\ s^2\log\frac{m_1}{m_0}
\]
by Mertens' theorem.
This closes the deficit at the full critical scale provided \(s\log(m_1/m_0)\gtrsim\log R\).
Notice, however, that the mechanism has an intrinsic cap: pigeonhole produces collisions only when the window overfills the \(\le 2p\) residue classes of the mod-\(p\) circle, so only primes below the window size are useful (the proof takes \(4m_1\le s\)).
The available weighted mass is therefore at most \(\sum_{p\le s}\log p/p\approx\log s\), and the deficit can be closed only if
\[
  s\log s\ \gtrsim\ \log R,
  \qquad\text{i.e.}\qquad
  s\ \gtrsim\ \frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
Since every branch of the proof returns \(M=O(s)\), we take \(s\) as small as this balance allows, and \(s\asymp\log R/\log\log R\) is forced.

The same accounting answers the second question: no fixed window size can work.
For constant \(s\), the total arithmetic obstruction a window can accumulate --- the norm gap plus all available collision divisibility --- is at most about \(s^2\log s\), a quantity independent of \(R\), while the geometric deficit \(s\log R\) grows without bound.
The strengthened window inequality therefore fails for every fixed \(s\) once \(R\) is large: within this framework, arcs of length \(C\sqrt R\) cannot be given a uniformly finite bound.
Finally, the missing primes are only conditionally available, since every prime in \((m_0,m_1]\) might instead divide \(N\).
The proof handles this with a dichotomy: a Chebyshev bound guarantees that each interval \((m_08^i,m_08^{i+1}]\) carries weighted prime mass at least \(\log2/2\), so across \(k\asymp\log\log R\) such intervals either the missing mass reaches the scale \(\log\log R\) demanded above, or some prime of controlled size divides \(N\) and descent applies, again yielding \(M=O(s)\).

\section{Related Work and the Logarithmic Baseline}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Classical convex-curve bounds}

Jarnik's theorem on lattice points on convex curves gives the correct order of magnitude \(O(L^{2/3})\) for lattice points on a strictly convex arc of length \(L\) \cite{Jarnik1926}.
Applied directly to a circular arc of length \(L=C\sqrt R\), this gives only \(O_C(R^{1/3})\).
Bombieri and Pila's determinant method gives far-reaching bounds for integral points on arcs and ovals \cite{BombieriPila1989}; our proof is also determinant-driven, but it uses the special arithmetic of a fixed circle and the Gaussian integers.
The identity at its core is the Gaussian-integer case of a Vandermonde identity of Ramana \cite{Ramana2007}, introduced there with precisely such applications in view: integer points on small arcs of conics, and divisors of an integer in prescribed congruence classes.

\subsection{Cilleruelo--Cordoba and Cilleruelo--Granville}
\label{subsec:cc-log}

For circles centered at the origin, much sharper local results are known.
Cilleruelo and Cordoba proved fixed-\(k\) bounds below the square-root scale \cite{CillerueloCordoba1992}.
A convenient form is: for every \(k\ge 1\), every arc of length
\[
  \sqrt2\,R^{1/2-\frac{1}{4\floor{k/2}+2}}
\]
contains at most \(k\) lattice points.
Cilleruelo and Granville later studied the fine structure of close lattice points on circles and gave families of four points at short scales \cite{CillerueloGranville2009}; related conjectures on boundedness of short arcs appear in \cite{CillerueloGranville2007}.

The Cilleruelo--Cordoba estimates imply a logarithmic bound at the critical \(\sqrt R\)-scale.
Assume \(R>e^2\), and set
\[
  k_R=\ceil{\frac{\log R}{2}}.
\]
Then
\[
  4\floor{k_R/2}+2\ge \log R
  \implies
  \frac{1}{4\floor{k_R/2}+2}\le \frac1{\log R}.
\]
%Indeed, if \(k_R=2q\), then \(2q\ge \frac12\log R\), so
%\(4q+2\ge \log R+2\).  If \(k_R=2q+1\), then
%\(2q+1\ge \frac12\log R\), so \(4q+2\ge \log R\). 
The length allowed by the Cilleruelo--Cordoba theorem is at least
\[
  \sqrt2\,R^{1/2-\frac{1}{4\floor{k_R/2}+2}}
  \ge
  \sqrt2\,R^{1/2-\frac1{\log R}}
  =
  \frac{\sqrt2}{e}\sqrt R.
\]
Thus arcs of length at most \((\sqrt2/e)\sqrt R\) contain at most
\[
  k_R\le \frac12\log R+1
\]
lattice points.

For a general fixed \(C>0\), divide an arc of length at most \(C\sqrt R\) into
\[
  J_C=\ceil{\frac{Ce}{\sqrt2}}
\]
subarcs, each of length at most \((\sqrt2/e)\sqrt R\).
Summing the preceding bound over the subarcs gives
\[
  \#(I\cap\Z^2)
  \le
  J_C\left(\frac12\log R+1\right)
  =
  O_C(\log R).
\]
Theorem~\ref{thm:geometric} replaces this \(O_C(\log R)\) baseline by
\[
  O_C\!\left(\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}\right).
\]

\subsection{Comparison with the Cilleruelo--Cordoba argument}
\label{subsec:comparison}

It's worth comparing the present proof to the Cilleruelo--Cordoba argument behind the fixed-\(k\) bounds, because at bottom they are the same kind of argument: both are integrality-gap arguments in \(\GI\), isolating a quantity that is a nonzero Gaussian integer --- hence has norm at least \(1\) --- and that is forced to be small when many lattice points crowd into a short arc.
The difference lies in which quantity carries the gap and, decisively, in which primes are allowed to strengthen it.

In \cite{CillerueloCordoba1992} the gap is attached to pairs of points through the multiplicative structure of \(\E_N\).
Writing the split part of \(N\) as \(\prod_j p_j^{\alpha_j}\), every point of \(\E_N\) has the form \(\sqrt N\,e^{2\pi i(\sum_j\gamma_j\Phi_j+t/4)}\), where \(\Phi_j\) is the angle of the Gaussian prime above \(p_j\) and the integer exponents satisfy \(|\gamma_j|\le\alpha_j\) and \(\gamma_j\equiv\alpha_j\pmod2\).
The half-angle difference of two points is then, up to a unit, the angle of a lattice point on a circle of radius at most \(\sqrt2\prod_j p_j^{|\gamma_j-\gamma_j'|/4}\); being off the real axis, that point has imaginary part at least \(1\), so the angular gap between the two points is at least the reciprocal of this descended radius.
Multiplying the pair bound over all \(\binom{m+1}2\) pairs and maximizing the exponent sums \(\sum_{s,s'}|\gamma_{j,s}-\gamma_{j,s'}|\) by an extremal combinatorial argument yields the fixed-\(k\) theorem.

Notice that at the level of a single window the two mechanisms are quantitatively identical.
The bare determinant obstruction (Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical}) shows that \(2s+1\) points cannot all lie on an arc of length below \(R^{1/2-1/(4s+2)}\) --- with \(k=2s\) this matches the Cilleruelo--Cordoba length \(\sqrt2\,R^{1/2-1/(4\floor{k/2}+2)}\) exponent for exponent!
The Ramana determinant should therefore be viewed as a repackaging of the same integrality gap: the Vandermonde factorization collects all pairwise differences of a window into a single nonzero Gaussian integer, and the norm bound \(\Norm(J_s)\ge1\) is applied once per window instead of once per pair.
In bare form the repackaging buys nothing --- both methods saturate at the \(O(\log R)\) baseline of Section~\ref{subsec:cc-log}, and in both the loss enters at the same step, namely a uniform per-pair bound imposed simultaneously on all pairs of a cluster, though only extremal pairs can attain it.

What the repackaging does buy is a place for arithmetic from outside the factorization of \(N\).
The angular parameterization reads everything off the split primes dividing \(N\); primes not dividing \(N\) are invisible to it.
The determinant, being a polynomial in the coordinates of the points, also feels congruences of the ambient lattice: reduction modulo a prime \(p\nmid N\) crowds a window into at most \(2p\) residue classes, and every collision contributes a factor of \(p\) to \(J_s\) (Section~\ref{sec:mainresult}).
The two proofs therefore draw on complementary prime sets --- divisors of \(N\) there, non-divisors of \(N\) here --- and it is this second, unconditional supply of primes that moves the barrier from \(\log R\) to \(\log R/\log\log R\).
The descent branch of the present proof, in which a prime dividing \(N\) maps the family to a strictly smaller circle, is the direct analogue of the Cilleruelo--Cordoba observation that angle differences are realized on smaller circles, used dynamically rather than pointwise.
Below the critical exponent the comparison reverses: the fixed-\(k\) bounds give uniformly finite counts for arcs of length \(R^\alpha\) with \(\alpha<1/2\), where the present theorem says nothing new.

\subsection{The \(p\)-adic and global determinant methods}
\label{subsec:padic}

There is a second family of determinant methods that our proof should be measured against.
Heath-Brown's \(p\)-adic determinant method \cite{HeathBrown2002} replaces the archimedean input of Bombieri--Pila --- points that are close on a short arc make the monomial determinant small --- with a non-archimedean one: points of a variety lying in a common residue class modulo an auxiliary prime \(p\) are \(p\)-adically close, so the determinant is divisible by a large power of \(p\).
If the divisibility exceeds the height bound, the determinant must vanish, the whole residue class lies on an auxiliary hypersurface of controlled degree, and one concludes by induction and B\'ezout.
Salberger's global determinant method \cite{Salberger2023} runs this argument at many primes simultaneously, and Walsh \cite{Walsh2015} sharpened the bookkeeping to remove the \(B^\varepsilon\) loss for curves.

Written in this language, the missing-prime mechanism of Section~\ref{sec:mainresult} is a global determinant argument on a fixed circle.
The arc supplies the archimedean smallness, residue collisions at the missing primes supply the divisibility, and the nonzero integer \(\Norm(J_s)\) must pay both bills at once; condition \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition} is exactly the resulting inequality.
The differences are instructive.
Our auxiliary primes are tiny --- below the window size, where pigeonhole forces collisions across the \(\sim p\) residue classes --- whereas Heath-Brown takes \(p\) comparable to a power of the height and works one class at a time.
Primes dividing \(N\), which the general method has no use for, get a separate mechanism here: descent in \(\GI\), which is special to norm forms.
And the structural half of Heath-Brown's dichotomy is closed on the circle: in the norm-adapted basis the determinant never vanishes (Proposition~\ref{prop:ramana}), so there is no auxiliary-hypersurface escape to exploit.
Could this machinery strengthen the theorem?
Not the shape, as far as we can tell.
The deeper \(p\)-adic filtration --- collisions modulo \(p^2,p^3,\ldots\) --- is a convergent constant gain, the pigeonhole cap of Section~\ref{subsec:proof-idea} binds the global method exactly as it binds ours, and the large-prime one-class analysis reproduces the below-critical fixed-\(k\) bounds rather than improving the critical scale.
We view the present proof as the small-prime, collision-based corner of the global determinant method, with descent as the extra arithmetic input.

\section{Main Results}\label{sec:mainresult}

This section states the principal ingredients; full proofs are deferred to the appendix.
Where a definition or condition might look unmotivated, we try to say where it comes from.

\subsection{The Ramana determinant}

Let \(s\ge 1\), and let \(z_0,\dots,z_{2s}\in\GI\).
Define the \((2s+1)\times(2s+1)\) matrix
\[
  \mathcal R_s(z)_{r,j} =
  \begin{cases}
    \conj{z_j}^{\,s-r}, & 0\le r<s,\\
    1, & r=s,\\
    z_j^{\,r-s}, & s<r\le 2s.
  \end{cases}
\]
Let
\[
  J_s(z_0,\ldots,z_{2s})=\det \mathcal R_s(z).
\]
The following identity is the case \(A=\GI\), \(\alpha_j=\conj{z_j}\), \(\beta_j=z_j\), \(\gamma=N\) of Theorem~1 of Ramana \cite{Ramana2007}.

\begin{proposition}[Ramana determinant identity]\label{prop:ramana}
If \(z_j\conj z_j=N\) for every \(j\), then
\[
  \left(\prod_{j=0}^{2s}z_j^s\right)J_s(z_0,\ldots,z_{2s})
  =
  N^{s(s+1)/2}\prod_{0\le i<j\le 2s}(z_j-z_i).
\]
If the \(z_j\) are distinct, then \(J_s\ne 0\).
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Subcritical block obstruction]\label{cor:subcritical}
Let \(z_0,\dots,z_{2s}\in\GI\) be distinct and satisfy \(z_j\conj z_j=N>0\).
If \(\Norm(z_i-z_j)\le B\) for every \(i<j\), then
\[
  N^{s^2}\le B^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
Consequently, if \(B^{s(2s+1)}<N^{s^2}\), some pair in the block has squared distance \(>B\).
\end{corollary}

\subsection{Sliding windows}

\begin{lemma}[Window-to-cardinality bound]\label{lem:window}
Let
\[
  t_0\le t_1\le\cdots\le t_{M-1}
\]
lie in an interval of length \(L\).
Suppose \(A>0\), \(s\ge 1\), and every consecutive window of \(2s+1\) indices has span greater than \(A\):
\[
  t_{j+2s}-t_j>A\qquad (0\le j<M-2s).
\]
Then
\[
  M\le 2s+\frac{2sL}{A}.
\]
\end{lemma}

Together with Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical}, this proves a bound whenever the determinant inequality is already subcritical at the original scale.

\subsection{Missing primes}

For interval endpoints \(m_0<m_1\), let
\[
  \mathcal P_N(m_0,m_1)=\{p\text{ prime}:m_0<p\le m_1,\ p\nmid N\}
\]
be the set of missing primes in the interval \((m_0,m_1]\), and define the weighted missing-prime mass
\[
  \M_N(m_0,m_1)
  =
  \sum_{p\in\mathcal P_N(m_0,m_1)}\frac{\log p}{p}.
\]

Fix a block \(z_0,\dots,z_{2s}\) of distinct points of \(\E_N\).
The missing-prime mechanism strengthens the lower bound \(\Norm(J_s)\ge1\) used in Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical} in three steps: residue collisions modulo each missing prime force divisibility of the chord products, the divisibility survives cancellation into the determinant, and the accumulated coprime prime powers raise the norm lower bound.

\begin{lemma}[Residue collisions]\label{lem:collision}
Let \(p\) be prime.
The congruence \(x^2+y^2\equiv N\pmod p\) has at most \(2p\) solutions in \((\Z/p\Z)^2\).
Consequently, if
\[
  c_p=\#\{(i,j):0\le i<j\le 2s,\ z_i\equiv z_j \pmod p\},
\]
where the congruence means both coordinates agree modulo \(p\), then
\[
  c_p\ \ge\ \frac{(2s+1)^2}{4p}-\frac{2s+1}{2}.
\]
If moreover \(4p\le s\), then \(c_p\ge s^2/(2p)\).
\end{lemma}

\begin{proposition}[Determinant divisibility]\label{prop:divisibility}
Let \(p\nmid N\) be prime.
Then \(p^{c_p}\mid J_s(z_0,\ldots,z_{2s})\) in \(\GI\), and hence \(p^{2c_p}\mid\Norm(J_s)\) in \(\Z\).
If \(P\) is any finite set of such primes, then
\[
  \prod_{p\in P}p^{2c_p}\ \le\ \Norm(J_s).
\]
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[Enhanced block obstruction]\label{cor:enhanced}
Let \(z_0,\dots,z_{2s}\in\GI\) be distinct with \(z_j\conj z_j=N>0\), and let \(P\) be a finite set of primes not dividing \(N\).
If \(\Norm(z_i-z_j)\le B\) for every \(i<j\), then
\[
  N^{s^2}\prod_{p\in P}p^{2c_p}\ \le\ B^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
\end{corollary}

It remains to convert Corollary~\ref{cor:enhanced} into a condition that can be checked from \(N\), \(s\), and the scales alone, and this is where the shape of the weighted-log inequality comes from.
Taking logarithms, Corollary~\ref{cor:enhanced} says that a window in which every chord norm is at most \(B\) is impossible as soon as
\[
  \underbrace{s(2s+1)\log B}_{\text{chord budget}}
  \;<\;
  \underbrace{s^2\log N}_{\text{norm gain}}
  \;+\;
  \underbrace{\sum_{p\in P}2c_p\log p}_{\text{collision gain}}.
\]
The left side is the total logarithmic size of all \(s(2s+1)\) chord norms; the first term on the right is the bare Ramana obstruction of Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical}; the second is the missing-prime enhancement.
Now replace each quantity by the bound certified by the preceding results, for a window of parameter span \(A\):
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item chord norms are rational integers of size at most \(A^2\), so \(B=\floor{A^2}\);
\item for \(P=\mathcal P_N(m_0,m_1)\) with \(4m_1\le s\), Lemma~\ref{lem:collision} certifies \(c_p\ge s^2/(2p)\ge E_p\coloneq\floor{s^2/(2p)}\);
\item rounding each exponent to an integer loses at most one collision, i.e.\ at most \(2\log p\) of gain: \(2\floor{s^2/(2p)}\ge s^2/p-2\), whence
\[
  \sum_{p\in P}2E_p\log p
  \;\ge\;
  s^2\sum_{p\in P}\frac{\log p}{p}-2\sum_{p\in P}\log p
  \;=\;
  s^2\M_N(m_0,m_1)-2\sum_{p\in P}\log p .
\]
\end{itemize}
In other words, the weighting by \(\log p/p\) is not an ansatz: a missing prime \(p\) produces about \(s^2/(2p)\) collisions in the window, each worth \(2\log p\) of divisibility, so the gain contributed by \(p\) per unit of \(s^2\) is exactly its Mertens mass \(\log p/p\).
Substituting the three certified bounds into the displayed inequality yields the finite weighted-log condition
\[
  s(2s+1)\log \floor{A^2}
  <
  s^2\M_N(m_0,m_1)-2\sum_{\substack{m_0<p\le m_1\\p\nmid N}}\log p
  +s^2\log N ,
  \tag{MP}\label{eq:missing-prime-condition}
\]
which is thus nothing more than the logarithmic form of Corollary~\ref{cor:enhanced} with every term replaced by its certified value.
The exact Lean statement uses natural-number floors and coercions; this display is its mathematical content.

\begin{remark}[Reading \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition} at the critical
scale]\label{rem:mp-critical} Substitute \(N=R^2\) and \(A=C\sqrt R\), so that \(\log\floor{A^2}\le\log R+2\log C\), and divide by \(s^2\).
Then \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition} is implied by
\[
  \M_N(m_0,m_1)
  \;>\;
  \frac{\log R}{s}+4\log C+o(1),
\]
where the \(o(1)\) collects the floor toll \(2\sum_{p\in P}\log p/s^2=O(\log m_1/s)\) and a \((2\log C)/s\) term; up to these lower-order terms, \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition} also requires this.
So the condition says precisely that the weighted missing-prime mass must beat the per-\(s^2\) deficit \((\log R)/s\) identified in Section~\ref{subsec:proof-idea}.
With \(s\asymp\log R/\log\log R\) the demand is of size \(\log\log R\), which is what the prime stack of the next subsection supplies.
\end{remark}

\begin{proposition}[Missing-prime branch]\label{prop:missing}
Let \(s\ge1\), let \(m_0<m_1\) with \(4m_1\le s\), and let \(A>0\) with \(\floor{A^2}\ge1\).
Assume the family \((z_i,t_i)_{i<M}\) satisfies the structural hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:param}: distinct points of \(\E_N\), monotone parameters dominating chord lengths, and parameters lying in an interval of length \(L\).
If \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition} holds, then every window of \(2s+1\) consecutive points has parameter span greater than \(A\), and consequently
\[
  M\le 2s+\frac{2sL}{A}.
\]
\end{proposition}

In Lean this is
\[
\texttt{card\_le\_of\_missing\_prime\_}\\
\texttt{interval\_weighted\_log\_bound}
\]
in \texttt{GaussianChain/MissingPrimeIntervalBranch.lean}.

\subsection{Prime extraction and descent}

Let \(m_0\ge 2\), \(k>0\), and set
\[
  m_1=m_0\,8^k.
\]
The prime-stack condition is:
\[
  \M_N(m_0,m_1)<k\,\frac{\log 2}{2}.
  \tag{FP}\label{eq:few-primes}
\]
Using Chebyshev's lower bound on prime mass in the intervals
\[
  m_0 8^i < p \le m_0 8^{i+1},
\]
condition \eqref{eq:few-primes} forces at least one prime divisor
\[
  m_0<p\le m_1,\qquad p\mid N.
\]

\begin{proposition}[Descent branch]\label{prop:descent}
Let \(p\mid N\) be a prime with \(m_0<p\le m_1\).
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item If \(p\equiv 3\pmod 4\), every \(z\in\E_N\) is divisible by \(p\) in \(\GI\), and division by \(p\) maps the family into \(\E_{N/p^2}\).
Chord lengths are divided by \(p\).
\item If \(p\equiv 1\pmod 4\), write \(p=\pi\conj\pi\) in \(\GI\).
At least half the points are divisible by one of \(\pi,\conj\pi\).
Dividing that subfamily by the corresponding Gaussian prime maps it into \(\E_{N/p}\).
Chord lengths are divided by \(\sqrt p\).
\end{enumerate}
If the corresponding descended subcritical inequalities hold, the original family satisfies a cardinality bound of size \(O(s)\).
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Finite two-scale theorem}

The formal finite theorem packages the preceding alternatives with split and inert scales.
In the split branch we deliberately use a constant-enlarged subcritical scale:
\[
  A_{\mathrm{split}}(p)=48\,\frac{C\sqrt R}{\sqrt p},
  \qquad
  A_{\mathrm{inert}}(p)=\frac{C\sqrt R}{p}.
\]
The factor \(48\) is not intrinsic.
It is a fixed convenience constant: it shrinks the split sliding-window length term by \(1/48\), while the endpoint subcritical inequality only has to absorb the fixed extra term \(2\log48\).

\begin{theorem}[Finite two-scale theorem]\label{thm:finite}
Let \(R^2=N\), let \(C>0\), and let the parametrized family satisfy the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:param}.
Suppose \(s,m_0,k,B\) satisfy, with \(m_1=m_0 8^k\):
\[
  s\le B\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R},
\]
\[
  4m_1\le s,
\]
the Chebyshev error bounds on the stack intervals, the missing-prime threshold inequality, the split endpoint subcritical inequality with \(48C\) in place of \(C\), and the inert endpoint subcritical inequality with \(C\).
Then
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}B\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{theorem}

This is the theorem named
\[
\texttt{card\_le\_49\_div\_12\_mul\_log\_div\_loglog\_}\\
\texttt{of\_radius\_sq\_twoScale\_endpoint\_bounds}
\]
in \texttt{GaussianChain/MainSublogBound.lean}.

\subsection{Asymptotic parameters}
\label{subsec:asymptotic}

Write
\[
  W=\log R,\qquad \Lambda=\log W.
\]
Given \(D>49\), set \(B=12D/49>12\) and choose the parameter schedule
\[
  \tilde B=\frac{B+12}{2},\qquad
  \beta=1+\frac{\tilde B}{12},\qquad
  \alpha=\frac{2+\beta}{2},
\]
\[
  s=\ceil{\tilde B\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}},\qquad
  m_0=\floor{\sqrt W},\qquad
  k=\floor{\frac{\Lambda}{\alpha\log 8}},\qquad
  m_1=m_0\,8^k.
\]
Then \(12<\tilde B<B\) and \(2<\alpha<\beta\) with \(6\beta=6+\tilde B/2< \tilde B\).
The appendix verifies all hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:finite} for sufficiently large \(R\); the three estimates that matter are the following.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=2em]
\item \emph{Deficit.}  From \(\tilde BW\le s\Lambda\) we get \(sW\le s^2\Lambda/\tilde B\), and the ceiling loss is absorbed because \(\tilde B<B\), so \(s\le BW/\Lambda\) eventually.
\item \emph{Stack gain.}  \(8^k\le W^{1/\alpha}\), so \(m_1\le W^{1/2+1/\alpha}\) with \(1/2+1/\alpha<1\); hence \(4m_1\le s\) eventually.
The floor loss in \(k\) is eventually dominated, \(k\ge\Lambda/(\beta\log8)\), so
\[
  k\,\frac{\log2}{2}\ \ge\ \frac{\Lambda}{6\beta}.
\]
\item \emph{Threshold.}  Since \(6\beta<\tilde B\), the margin
\[
  \frac{1}{6\beta}-\frac{1}{\tilde B}
  =
  \frac{\tilde B-12}{\tilde B\,(\tilde B+12)}
\]
is a fixed positive constant, and the endpoint and fixed-\(C\) error terms are eventually smaller than this margin times \(s^2\Lambda\).
By Remark~\ref{rem:mp-critical}, the stack gain then forces \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition} in the missing branch.
\end{itemize}
Theorem~\ref{thm:finite} with this schedule yields
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}\,B\,\frac W\Lambda=D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}
\]
for every \(D>49\), which is Theorem~\ref{thm:param}.

\subsubsection*{The formalized instance}

The Lean development instantiates the schedule at the fixed values
\[
  \tilde B=\frac{97}{8},\qquad
  \alpha=\frac{2001}{1000},\qquad
  \beta=\frac{201}{100},
\]
which satisfy \(6\beta=12.06<12.125=\tilde B\).
The stack estimate becomes
\[
  m_1=m_0 8^k\le W^{1/2}W^{1000/2001}=W^{4001/4002},
\]
so \(4m_1\le s\) eventually since \(\Lambda=o(W^{1/4002})\); the floor estimate
\[
  \floor{\frac{\Lambda}{(2001/1000)\log8}}
  \ge
  \frac{\Lambda}{(201/100)\log8}
\]
holds for large \(R\) and gives the stack gain \(k\log2/2\ge \Lambda/(6\beta)=(50/603)\Lambda\); and the error terms are budgeted as
\[
  2(m_1+1)\log m_1\le \frac{1}{8192}s^2\Lambda,
  \qquad
  s(2s+1)\cdot 2\log C\le \frac{3}{524288}s^2\Lambda,
\]
so that the exact numerical comparison used in Lean is
\[
  \frac{8}{97}+\frac1{8192}+\frac{3}{524288}
  <
  \frac{50}{603}.
\]
For the enlarged split scale the same endpoint estimate is applied with \(48C\) in place of \(C\), which only changes the fixed constant term from \(2\log C\) to \(2\log(48C)\).
Because \(\alpha,\beta\) are fixed rather than chosen as functions of \(D\), the certified range is \(B>97/8\), i.e.
\[
  D>\frac{49}{12}\cdot\frac{97}{8}=\frac{4753}{96}=49.51\ldots,

about one percent above the optimum of the general schedule.
Taking \(B=600/49\) gives the concrete formalized corollary
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}\cdot \frac{600}{49}\,\frac{W}{\Lambda}
  =
  50\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]

\subsection{Limits of the constant}
\label{subsec:constant-limits}

How much smaller can \(D\) get?
Three further sharpenings reduce the constant without changing the architecture.
None of them is formalized; we record each with its yield.

\emph{(a) Saturated windows: \(D>48\).}  The sliding-window bound \(M\le 2s+2sL/A\) is wasteful when \(A\ge L\): if every window of \(2s+1\) consecutive points has parameter span greater than \(A\), while all parameters lie in an interval of length \(L\le A\), then no complete window can exist at all, so \(M\le 2s\).
Applying this in the missing branch (where \(A=C\sqrt R\ge L\)) and, on the descended circles, in the inert and split branches (taking the descended scale equal to the descended length, which also eliminates the convenience factor \(48\)) gives \(M\le 2s\) in the missing and inert branches and \(M\le 4s\) in the split branch, whose divisibility-class argument still costs a factor \(2\).
The branch factor \(49/12\) improves to \(4\), and \(D>4\cdot12=48\).

\emph{(b) Mertens in place of Chebyshev: \(D>8\).}  The stack certifies only \(\log2/2\) of weighted mass per interval \((m_08^i,m_08^{i+1}]\), whose true mass is \(\log8\): an efficiency of \(1/6\), and the source of the \(6\) in the gain \(\Lambda/(6\beta)\).
With Mertens' theorem \(\sum_{p\le x}\log p/p=\log x+O(1)\) the dichotomy simplifies: taking \(m_0=W^{\theta}\) and \(m_1=W^{\theta_1}\) with \(\theta_1<1\), either no prime of \((m_0,m_1]\) divides \(N\), in which case the missing mass is \((\theta_1-\theta)\Lambda+O(1)\), or descent applies.
The missing branch then needs \(B>1/(\theta_1-\theta)\), while the descent endpoint inequalities need \(B>1/\theta\); optimizing \(\theta=1/2\), \(\theta_1\to1\) gives \(B>2\), hence, with (a), \(D>8\).
This also identifies \(m_0=\sqrt W\) as the optimal descent scale, since \(\max(1/\theta,\,1/(\theta_1-\theta))\ge2\) for every choice of \(\theta<\theta_1<1\).

\emph{(c) Tight collision counting: \(D>5\).}  Lemma~\ref{lem:collision} gives away two factors of \(2\).
The mod-\(p\) circle has exactly \(p-(-1)^{(p-1)/2}\le p+1\) points rather than at most \(2p\), and in the range \(p\le m_1=o(s)\) the Cauchy--Schwarz count gives \(c_p\ge(2-o(1))\,s^2/p\) rather than \(s^2/(2p)\).
The collision gain per prime improves from \(s^2\log p/p\) to \((4-o(1))\,s^2\log p/p\), the missing branch needs only \(B>1/(4(\theta_1-\theta))\), and the balance against the descent requirement \(B>1/\theta\) moves to \(\theta=4/5\): \(B>5/4\), hence \(D>5\).

Within the two-scale architecture --- one window scale, one descent --- we don't know how to go below \(D>5\): the remaining losses are the factor \(2\) in the split class count and the \(\max(1/\theta,\,1/(4(\theta_1-\theta)))\ge 5/4\) balance, both of which appear intrinsic to a single descent.
Going further seems to require iterated descent or the structural inputs discussed in Section~\ref{sec:future}.

\section{Conclusion and Restriction Consequences}

To recap, the theorem gives a formalized improvement from the Cilleruelo--Cordoba logarithmic baseline
\[
  O_C(\log R)
\]
to
\[
  O_C\!\left(\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}\right)
\]
for arcs of length \(C\sqrt R\).

The proof stays entirely inside the integrality-gap framework of Section~\ref{subsec:comparison}: no exponential sums, no equidistribution input, and no unproven hypotheses.
What is new is the bookkeeping --- a determinant that can absorb congruence information at primes not dividing \(N\), and a dichotomy that converts the absence of such primes into descent to a smaller circle.
Every step, from the determinant identity through the Chebyshev prime-stack estimates to the final parameter choice with explicit constants, is machine-checked in Lean~4 over Mathlib \cite{Mathlib2020,deMouraUllrich2021}.

The result also supplies a quantitative input for restriction estimates on \(\T^2\).
The frequency set of an eigenfunction with eigenvalue \(4\pi^2N\) is exactly \(\E_N\), and the difficult local contribution to restriction kernels comes from pairs of nearly parallel frequencies, that is, frequencies lying in short arcs of \(\E_N\).
Any restriction argument whose local loss is controlled by the maximal number of lattice points in arcs of length \(C\sqrt R\) may therefore replace a logarithmic loss by \(\log R/\log\log R\), in a machine-checked form.
We emphasize that this is an arithmetic input to such arguments, not a standalone restriction theorem: converting the cluster bound into an operator-norm estimate requires the analytic framework, for instance that of Bourgain and Rudnick \cite{BourgainRudnick2009}, in which the cluster number appears.

\section{Future Work}
\label{sec:future}

Can the method of this paper be pushed all the way to the conjectured uniform bound \cite{CillerueloGranville2007}, under which arcs of length \(C\sqrt R\) contain \(O_C(1)\) lattice points?
We believe the answer is no.  In this section we record what the framework can still give, and where it stops.

Within the framework, the remaining room is in the constants, and Section~\ref{subsec:constant-limits} quantifies it: saturated windows, Mertens' theorem in place of the Chebyshev stack, and tight collision counting bring the admissible constant from \(49\) down to \(D>5\).
Collisions modulo prime powers \(p^j\le s\) add a further convergent gain.
None of this changes the shape \(\log R/\log\log R\).

The shape is pinned down by two caps, and it's worth stating both, because together they close off the apparent sources of slack.
First, the arithmetic cap: collisions modulo \(p\) require the window to overfill the roughly \(p\) residue classes of the mod-\(p\) circle, so no prime beyond the window size \(s\) can contribute, and even with exact collision counting the total divisibility gain of a window is at most about \(s^2\sum_{p\le s}\log p/p\approx s^2\log s\).
Second, the geometric cap: the uniform chord bound \(B=\floor{A^2}\), which looks like the lossiest step of the proof, is not the bottleneck.
Replacing it by the exact graded chord profile of an arc --- pairs at parameter distance \(\delta\) have chord norm at most \(\delta^2\) --- improves the geometric side of the window inequality by only \(\Theta(s^2)\): for \(2s+1\) equally spaced parameters, the product of all pairwise gaps is within \(e^{O(s^2)}\) of the uniform bound.
Both caps are \(O(s^2\log s)\), while the deficit of the critical scale is \(s\log R\) (Section~\ref{subsec:proof-idea}).
Any argument that proceeds by a per-window integrality gap strengthened by congruence divisibility therefore needs \(s\log s\gtrsim\log R\), and since its output is \(M=O(s)\), it cannot go below \(\log R/\log\log R\).
In particular, a finite bound cannot be reached by refining the counting in this paper; it will require a genuinely new mechanism.

A plausible common feature of such a mechanism is that it must see the rigidity of an entire cluster at once, rather than pairwise or windowwise integrality.
Three directions seem worth pursuing.
The first is the structure theory of close lattice points of Cilleruelo and Granville \cite{CillerueloGranville2009}, which treats a hypothetical \((k+1)\)-point cluster as a solution of an explicit Diophantine system and classifies the small cases; making that classification quantitative at the critical scale is the most direct route to uniform boundedness.
The second is a hybrid of determinant divisibility with the multiplicative parameterization of \cite{CillerueloCordoba1992}: the angular method has sharp per-pair bounds but aggregates them lossily, and the extremal exponent configurations that saturate its combinatorial step are attained by points that do not lie on a common short arc; ruling such configurations out with congruence information is an unexplored middle ground.
The third is to use several prime divisors of \(N\) at once: the descent branch consumes a single prime, while the conjectured obstruction should strengthen with every split prime of \(N\).
On the formal side, each of these begins with ingredients --- character-sum evaluations of the mod-\(p\) circle, the Cilleruelo--Granville systems, iterated descent --- that sit within reach of the existing Lean development.

\section{Proof of the Determinant and Subcritical Bounds}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:ramana}]
The \(j\)-th column of \(\mathcal R_s\) is
\[
  (\conj z_j^s,\conj z_j^{s-1},\ldots,\conj z_j,1,z_j,\ldots,z_j^s)^T.
\]
Multiply this column by \(z_j^s\).
Because \(z_j\conj z_j=N\), the entries become
\[
  (N^s,N^{s-1}z_j,\ldots,Nz_j^{s-1},z_j^s,z_j^{s+1},\ldots,z_j^{2s})^T.
\]
Factor \(N^{s-r}\) from the row corresponding to \(\conj z^{s-r}\), for \(r=0,\dots,s-1\).
The total factored power is
\[
  N^{1+2+\cdots+s}=N^{s(s+1)/2}.
\]
The remaining matrix is the ordinary Vandermonde matrix with columns
\[
  (1,z_j,z_j^2,\ldots,z_j^{2s})^T.
\]
Its determinant is
\[
  \prod_{0\le i<j\le 2s}(z_j-z_i).
\]
This proves the identity.
If the \(z_j\) are distinct, the Vandermonde product is nonzero in the domain \(\GI\), and the product \(\prod_j z_j^s\) is nonzero because \(N>0\).
Hence \(J_s\ne0\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical}]
Take norms in Proposition~\ref{prop:ramana}.
Since \(J_s\) is a nonzero Gaussian integer,
\[
  \Norm(J_s)\ge 1.
\]
The norm of \(\prod_j z_j^s\) is
\[
  \prod_j \Norm(z_j)^s=N^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
The norm of the right side is
\[
  \Norm\!\left(N^{s(s+1)/2}\right)
  \prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i)
  =
  N^{s(s+1)}\prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i),
\]
because the rational integer \(N^{s(s+1)/2}\) has Gaussian norm \(N^{s(s+1)}\).
Thus
\[
  N^{s(2s+1)}
  \le
  N^{s(s+1)}\prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i),
\]
and hence
\[
  N^{s^2}\le \prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i).
\]
There are
\[
  \binom{2s+1}{2}=s(2s+1)
\]
pairs.
If every norm is at most \(B\), then
\[
  N^{s^2}\le B^{s(2s+1)}.
\]
The final assertion is the contrapositive.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:window}]
Let \(K=2s\).
The hypothesis gives
\[
  t_{j+K}-t_j>A\qquad (0\le j<M-K).
\]
Sum these inequalities over \(j=0,\dots,M-K-1\):
\[
  (M-K)A
  <
  \sum_{j=0}^{M-K-1}(t_{j+K}-t_j).
\]
Each \(t_i\) occurs with coefficient at most \(K\) in the telescoping difference between the final and initial portions of the sequence, and all \(t_i\) lie in an interval of length \(L\).
Hence
\[
  \sum_{j=0}^{M-K-1}(t_{j+K}-t_j)\le KL.
\]
Therefore
\[
  M-K < \frac{KL}{A},
\]
and so
\[
  M\le K+\frac{KL}{A}
  =
  2s+\frac{2sL}{A}.
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Missing-Prime and Descent Mechanisms}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Lemma~\ref{lem:collision}]
For each of the \(p\) values of \(x\in\Z/p\Z\), the congruence \(y^2\equiv N-x^2\pmod p\) is a quadratic equation over the field \(\Z/p\Z\) and has at most two roots \(y\).
Hence the mod-\(p\) circle has at most \(2p\) points.

Write \(n=2s+1\) and let \(\rho(z)=(\Re z\bmod p,\ \Im z\bmod p)\) denote coordinatewise reduction.
Since \(\Re(z_i)^2+\Im(z_i)^2=N\) in \(\Z\), the map \(\rho\) sends the block into the mod-\(p\) circle.
For each residue class \(b\) let \(f_b\) be the number of indices \(i\) with \(\rho(z_i)=b\), so that \(\sum_b f_b=n\) with at most \(2p\) nonzero terms.
By the Cauchy--Schwarz inequality,
\[
  n^2=\Bigl(\sum_b f_b\Bigr)^{\!2}\le 2p\sum_b f_b^2 .
\]
The number of ordered pairs \((i,j)\) with \(\rho(z_i)=\rho(z_j)\), the diagonal included, is \(\sum_b f_b^2\); removing the \(n\) diagonal pairs and halving,
\[
  c_p=\frac{\sum_b f_b^2-n}{2}\ \ge\ \frac{n^2}{4p}-\frac n2 .
\]
For the second claim, the inequality \(n^2/(4p)-n/2\ge s^2/(2p)\) is equivalent to \(n^2-2pn\ge 2s^2\).
From \(4p\le s\),
\[
  n^2-2pn\ \ge\ (2s+1)^2-\tfrac s2(2s+1)\ =\ 3s^2+\tfrac72 s+1\ \ge\ 2s^2 .
  \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:divisibility}]
If \(z_i\equiv z_j\pmod p\), both coordinates of \(z_j-z_i\) are divisible by \(p\), so \(p\mid z_j-z_i\) in \(\GI\).
The \(c_p\) colliding pairs index distinct factors of the Vandermonde product, whence
\[
  p^{c_p}\ \Big|\ \prod_{0\le i<j\le 2s}(z_j-z_i).
\]
By Proposition~\ref{prop:ramana},
\[
  \Bigl(\prod_j z_j^s\Bigr) J_s
  =
  N^{s(s+1)/2}\prod_{i<j}(z_j-z_i),
\]
so \(p^{c_p}\) divides \(u\,J_s\), where \(u=\prod_j z_j^s\).

We now cancel \(u\).
Its norm is \(\Norm(u)=N^{s(2s+1)}\), which is coprime to \(p\) because \(p\nmid N\) and \(p\) is prime.
In general, if \(u,d\in\GI\) with \(p\nmid\Norm(u)\), and \(p^e\mid u\,d\) in \(\GI\), then \(p^e\mid d\).
Indeed, multiplying by \(\conj u\) gives \(p^e\mid \conj u\,u\,d=\Norm(u)\,d\); a rational integer divides a Gaussian integer exactly when it divides both coordinates, so \(p^e\) divides \(\Norm(u)\Re(d)\) and \(\Norm(u)\Im(d)\) in \(\Z\); since \(\gcd(p^e,\Norm(u))=1\), it divides \(\Re(d)\) and \(\Im(d)\), that is, \(p^e\mid d\) in \(\GI\).
Applying this with \(d=J_s\) and \(e=c_p\) gives \(p^{c_p}\mid J_s\).

Taking norms, \(\Norm(p^{c_p})=p^{2c_p}\) divides \(\Norm(J_s)\).
For distinct primes the powers \(p^{2c_p}\) are pairwise coprime, so their product over \(P\) also divides \(\Norm(J_s)\).
Since the \(z_j\) are distinct, \(J_s\ne0\) by Proposition~\ref{prop:ramana}, so \(\Norm(J_s)\ge\prod_{p\in P}p^{2c_p}\).
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:enhanced}]
As in the proof of Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical}, taking norms in the determinant identity gives
\[
  N^{s(2s+1)}\,\Norm(J_s)
  =
  N^{s(s+1)}\prod_{i<j}\Norm(z_j-z_i)
  \le
  N^{s(s+1)}B^{s(2s+1)},
\]
hence \(N^{s^2}\Norm(J_s)\le B^{s(2s+1)}\).
Inserting the lower bound of Proposition~\ref{prop:divisibility} for \(\Norm(J_s)\) completes the proof.
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:missing}]
If \(M\le 2s\) the bound is trivial, so assume \(M>2s\).
Set \(P=\mathcal P_N(m_0,m_1)\), \(B=\floor{A^2}\), and \(E_p=\floor{s^2/(2p)}\).

Consider any window \(z_j,\dots,z_{j+2s}\) of consecutive points and suppose its parameter span satisfies \(t_{j+2s}-t_j\le A\).
For indices \(i<k\) in the window, monotonicity of the parameters gives \(0\le t_k-t_i\le A\), and the chord-domination hypothesis gives
\[
  \Norm(z_i-z_k)=|z_i-z_k|^2\le (t_k-t_i)^2\le A^2 .
\]
Chord norms are rational integers, so \(\Norm(z_i-z_k)\le B\) for every pair in the window.

Every \(p\in P\) satisfies \(4p\le 4m_1\le s\), so Lemma~\ref{lem:collision} gives \(c_p\ge s^2/(2p)\ge E_p\) for the collision counts of the window.
Corollary~\ref{cor:enhanced} applied to the window therefore forces
\[
  N^{s^2}\prod_{p\in P}p^{2E_p}
  \ \le\
  N^{s^2}\prod_{p\in P}p^{2c_p}
  \ \le\
  B^{s(2s+1)} .
\]
All quantities are at least \(1\), so we may take logarithms; using \(2E_p=2\floor{s^2/(2p)}\ge s^2/p-2\),
\[
  s(2s+1)\log B
  \ \ge\ s^2\log N+\sum_{p\in P}2E_p\log p
  \ \ge\ s^2\log N+s^2\M_N(m_0,m_1)-2\sum_{p\in P}\log p .
\]
This contradicts \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition}.
Hence every window of \(2s+1\) consecutive points has parameter span greater than \(A\), and Lemma~\ref{lem:window} gives
\[
  M\le 2s+\frac{2sL}{A}.
  \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Proposition~\ref{prop:descent}]
If \(p\equiv3\pmod4\), then \(-1\) is not a square modulo \(p\).
From
\[
  x^2+y^2\equiv0\pmod p
\]
we obtain \(x\equiv y\equiv0\pmod p\).
Hence \(p\mid z\) in \(\GI\) for every point \(z\), and \(z/p\in\GI\) satisfies
\[
  \Norm(z/p)=N/p^2.
\]
Moreover
\[
  |z_i/p-z_j/p|=|z_i-z_j|/p.
\]
Thus the parameter length in the descended problem is divided by \(p\).

If \(p\equiv1\pmod4\), then \(p=\pi\conj\pi\) in \(\GI\).
Since
\[
  p\mid z\conj z,
\]
at least one of \(\pi,\conj\pi\) divides \(z\).
Partition the original family according to which Gaussian prime divides \(z\); one class has at least half the points.
Divide that class by the corresponding Gaussian prime.
The norm drops from \(N\) to \(N/p\), and chord lengths are divided by
\[
  |\pi|=\sqrt p.
\]

In either case, once the descended determinant inequality is subcritical, Corollary~\ref{cor:subcritical} and Lemma~\ref{lem:window} apply on the descended circle.
In the split case the passage to the larger of two classes costs a genuine factor of \(2\).
The optimized finite theorem keeps this factor but reduces the split sliding-window length term by using a constant enlargement of the descended subcritical scale.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Finite Two-Scale Theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:finite}]
Recall \(m_1=m_0\,8^k\).
There are two cases.

If the missing-prime mass on \((m_0,m_1]\) is at least
\[
  k\,\frac{\log2}{2},
\]
then the threshold hypothesis implies the weighted missing-prime condition \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition}.
Proposition~\ref{prop:missing} gives
\[
  M\le 2s+\frac{2sL}{C\sqrt R}.
\]
Since \(L\le C\sqrt R\), this is at most \(4s\), and hence at most
\[
  \frac{49}{12}B\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}
\]
under the hypothesis \(s\le B\log R/\log\log R\).

If the missing-prime mass is smaller than \(k\log2/2\), the Chebyshev interval lower bound over the geometric stack implies that some prime \(p\mid N\) lies in \(m_0<p\le m_1\).
Apply Proposition~\ref{prop:descent}.
The finite theorem assumes the split endpoint inequality
\[
  s(2s+1)\log\left(\frac{(48C)^2R}{m_0}\right)
  <
  s^2(2\log R-\log m_0),
\]
and the inert endpoint inequality
\[
  s(2s+1)\log\left(\frac{C^2R}{m_0^2}\right)
  <
  s^2(2\log R-2\log m_0).
\]
Since \(p>m_0\), these endpoint inequalities imply the corresponding descended subcritical inequalities for every possible \(p\) in the stack.
The descended window bound gives at most \(4s\) points in the inert case.
In the split case we use the scale
\[
  48\,\frac{C\sqrt R}{\sqrt p}.
\]
The descended length is at most \(C\sqrt R/\sqrt p\), so the sliding-window length term is only \(1/48\) of the base term:
\[
  \frac{2s(C\sqrt R/\sqrt p)}{48C\sqrt R/\sqrt p}
  =
  \frac{2s}{48}.
\]
The descended half-subfamily therefore has size at most
\[
  2s+\frac{2s}{48}=\frac{49}{24}s.
\]
Multiplying by \(2\) for the split divisibility class gives
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}s.
\]
Thus in all cases
\[
  M\le \frac{49}{12}s\le \frac{49}{12}B\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
\]
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the Asymptotic Parameter Estimates}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:param}]
Let
\[
  W=\log R,\qquad \Lambda=\log W,
\]
both positive for sufficiently large \(R\).
Given \(D>49\), put \(B=12D/49>12\) and adopt the schedule of Section~\ref{subsec:asymptotic}:
\[
  \tilde B=\frac{B+12}2,\qquad
  \beta=1+\frac{\tilde B}{12},\qquad
  \alpha=\frac{2+\beta}2,
\]
\[
  s=\ceil{\tilde B\,\frac W\Lambda},\qquad
  m_0=\floor{\sqrt W},\qquad
  k=\floor{\frac{\Lambda}{\alpha\log8}},\qquad
  m_1=m_0\,8^k .
\]
From \(\tilde B>12\) we get \(\beta>2\), then \(2<\alpha<\beta\), and
\[
  6\beta=6+\frac{\tilde B}2<\tilde B .
\]
Eventually \(k>0\) and \(m_0\ge2\).
We verify the hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:finite}.

\emph{Scales.}  Since \(8^k\le e^{\Lambda/\alpha}=W^{1/\alpha}\),
\[
  m_1\le W^{1/2+1/\alpha},
  \qquad
  \frac12+\frac1\alpha<1 .
\]
Because \(s\ge\tilde BW/\Lambda\) and \(\Lambda=o(W^{1/2-1/\alpha})\), this gives \(4m_1\le s\) for all large \(R\).
In particular \(m_1=o(s)\) and \(\log m_1\le\Lambda\).

\emph{Ceiling.}  \(s\le\tilde BW/\Lambda+1\le BW/\Lambda\) eventually, because \((B-\tilde B)W/\Lambda\to\infty\).

\emph{Stack gain.}  Writing \(x=\Lambda/(\alpha\log8)\), the floor satisfies \(\floor x\ge x-1\ge(\alpha/\beta)\,x\) once \(x\ge\beta/(\beta-\alpha)\), so eventually
\[
  k\,\frac{\log2}2
  \ \ge\
  \frac{\Lambda}{\beta\log8}\cdot\frac{\log2}2
  =
  \frac{\Lambda}{6\beta}.
\]

\emph{Descent endpoints.}  Eventually \(\log m_0\ge\Lambda/4\) and \(2\log(48C)\le\Lambda/64\); together with \(\tilde BW\le s\Lambda\) and the margin
\[
  \frac1{\tilde B}+\frac3{64}<\frac1{12}+\frac3{64}<\frac14,
\]
the split and inert lower-endpoint inequalities hold for all large \(R\).

\emph{Missing-prime threshold.}  Set
\[
  \varepsilon
  =
  \frac12\left(\frac1{6\beta}-\frac1{\tilde B}\right)
  =
  \frac{\tilde B-12}{2\,\tilde B\,(\tilde B+12)}
  \;>\;0 .
\]
Eventually \(2(m_1+1)\log m_1\le\varepsilon\,s^2\Lambda\), because \(m_1\log m_1\le W^{1/2+1/\alpha}\Lambda\) while \(s^2\Lambda\ge\tilde B^2W^2/\Lambda\); and eventually \(s(2s+1)\cdot2\log(48C)\le\varepsilon\,s^2\Lambda\), because the left side is \(O_C(s^2)\).
Combining these with \(sW\le s^2\Lambda/\tilde B\),
\[
  sW+s(2s+1)\cdot2\log(48C)+2(m_1+1)\log m_1
  \ \le\
  \left(\frac1{\tilde B}+2\varepsilon\right)s^2\Lambda
  =
  \frac{s^2\Lambda}{6\beta}
  \ \le\
  s^2\,k\,\frac{\log2}2 ,
\]
so the certified stack mass \(k\log2/2\) exceeds the deficit \((\log R)/s\) plus all error terms.
By Remark~\ref{rem:mp-critical}, this forces \eqref{eq:missing-prime-condition} in the missing branch, which is the finite missing-prime threshold inequality.

All hypotheses of Theorem~\ref{thm:finite} are therefore satisfied, and
\[
  M\le\frac{49}{12}\,B\,\frac W\Lambda=D\,\frac{\log R}{\log\log R}.
  \qedhere
\]
\end{proof}

The formalized proof is this argument with the fixed values \(\tilde B=97/8\), \(\alpha=2001/1000\), \(\beta=201/100\), and explicit eventual thresholds in place of the asymptotic statements.
It uses the coarse bounds \(s\le 26\,W/\Lambda\) and \(m_1\le 26W\le W^2\) (hence \(\log m_1\le2\Lambda\)) for scale estimates, the stack bound \(m_1+1\le(65/256)\,s\) with \(s\ge8320\) to certify \(2(m_1+1)\log m_1\le s^2\Lambda/8192\), and the budgets \(2\log C\le\Lambda/524288\) and \(2\log(48C)\le\Lambda/64\).
These produce the margin \(8/97+1/8192+3/524288<50/603\) of Section~\ref{subsec:asymptotic} and certify every \(D>4753/96\).
